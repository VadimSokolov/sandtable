\begin{abstract}
Designing human-machine teams for contested ground operations means comparing many combinations of
control modality, formation, and autonomy against uncertain communications and electronic warfare
(EW). High-fidelity engines such as the Army's Government-furnished \emph{ProjectGL} render each
mission at or near real time, too slow for the thousands of Monte-Carlo replications a
design-of-experiments sweep or optimizer needs. We present \sandtable{}, a lightweight, pure-Python,
mission-level simulator that stands in for that engine inside the experiment inner loop.
\sandtable{} holds every entity (ground vehicles, uncrewed aircraft, sensor and command nodes) as
parallel NumPy arrays and advances a fixed one-second step that updates, in order, command tasking,
movement over trafficable terrain, range-limited sensing into a per-side shared picture, and
aimed-fire engagement; a C0--C5 communications/EW ladder adds tasking latency, drops messages, and
thins that shared picture. The command layer carries the teaming question: a direct operator is
modeled as a single capacity-limited server whose attention dilutes across the assets it supervises
and whose orders ride the degradable link, whereas supervisory autonomy runs on-platform at a steady
quality independent of the link. A whole mission is a pure function of (scenario, seed, parameters),
so runs are reproducible, cacheable, and trivially parallel, and one mission of up to 1800 steps
completes in about a tenth of a second on one CPU core. \sandtable{} plugs into a
Bayesian-optimization and DOE orchestrator; we drive \nRep{} replications across 147 designs (about
44,100 replications) on an HPC cluster, and the attrition kernel reproduces Lanchester's square law
to within \lanchMaxErr\%. Three studies yield decision-relevant results. First, the
direct-to-supervisory control optimum \emph{moves}: at a low span of control (one operator to two
assets) direct control wins only when communications are clear, while at high span (one-to-eight)
on-platform autonomy wins at every communications level, by \cpDeltaHiSpanClear{}~percentage points
even in the clear. Second, route choice traces a clean speed-survivability frontier: a defilade
route reaches \ucThreeSuccCover\% mission success against \ucThreeSuccFast\% for a fast exposed
corridor, at \ucThreeTempoRatio$\times$ the time. Third, a recon swarm cueing near-blind shooters
lifts success from \ucFiveSuccBlind\% (no recon) to \ucFiveSuccBestClear\%, but jamming degrades the
sensor-to-shooter chain and cuts that to \ucFiveSuccBestSevere\%. \sandtable{} is the fast
inner-loop companion to ProjectGL for Army mission engineering.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The U.S. Army's shift toward human-machine teams, dismounted squads working with robotic combat
vehicles, uncrewed aircraft, and sensor nodes, turns tactics into a design problem. A mission
engineer must decide how one operator's attention is divided across autonomous assets, whether
control should be direct or supervisory, which route a formation takes, and how large a recon
swarm should be, all under communications that an adversary actively degrades. Each of these is a
knob, and the knobs interact: the best control modality depends on the communications state, and
the value of a recon swarm depends on whether its picture survives jamming. Answering such
questions demands experiments that sweep the knobs and measure outcomes against an engineered
ground truth, not one-off demonstrations.

ProjectGL, the Army's Government-furnished simulation framework, is the authoritative,
high-fidelity environment for this work \citep{stanko2025projectgl}. It models vehicle dynamics,
sensing, and synthetic terrain in real time. That fidelity is exactly what makes it unsuitable for
the \emph{inner loop} of a design study:
each mission runs at or near real time, so a modest experiment of a few hundred design points, each
averaged over a few hundred stochastic replications, would take weeks of wall-clock time. Bayesian
optimization and sensitivity analysis need the objective evaluated thousands of times, quickly and
reproducibly. The fidelity that makes ProjectGL the right tool for crew-station development and
final validation makes it the wrong tool for searching a design space.

We resolve this with a two-tier workflow. A fast, low-fidelity, mission-level model searches the
design space and locates the decision-relevant regions; the high-fidelity engine then validates the
handful of designs that matter. This paper builds and exercises the fast tier. \sandtable{} is a
pure-Python simulator that keeps the mission-level effects that drive design decisions, movement
over trafficable terrain, probabilistic sensing and engagement, a two-tier command model, and a
communications/EW degradation ladder, while discarding the physics and rendering that dominate
high-fidelity cost. It follows the ``accurate enough, not high fidelity'' principle: the model is
kept only as detailed as it must be to produce the right \emph{ordering} of design alternatives.

A model built for a search loop must satisfy two properties that ordinary simulators often do not.
It must be a \emph{pure function} of its inputs, with no global or hidden state, so that a run is
reproducible from its seed, cacheable, and safe to evaluate in parallel. It must also carry an
\emph{engineered ground truth}, a known correct answer for each trial, so that outcomes are scored
against truth rather than against agreement with the model's own agents. \sandtable{} is designed
around both.

The contributions of this paper are the following.
\begin{enumerate}
\item \sandtable{}, a fast, reproducible, pure-Python mission-level simulator whose
\texttt{run\_mission} contract is a pure function of (scenario, seed, parameters), wired into a
Bayesian-optimization/DOE orchestrator and an HPC batch runner as the fast inner-loop companion to
ProjectGL (Section~\ref{sec:model}).
\item A control-modality model, direct human-in-the-loop versus supervisory autonomy, coupled to
span of control and a C0--C5 communications/EW ladder, that reproduces a \emph{moving optimum}: the
communications level at which autonomy overtakes human control falls as span rises
(Section~\ref{subsec:centerpiece}).
\item Three decision-relevant findings for Army human-machine teaming, the moving control optimum,
the speed-survivability frontier of route choice (Section~\ref{subsec:uc3}), and the EW-induced
collapse of a sensor-to-shooter chain (Section~\ref{subsec:uc5}), each generated from
\nRep{}-replication Monte-Carlo studies with confidence intervals.
\item An analytic validation: \sandtable{}'s emergent attrition matches Lanchester's square law to
within \lanchMaxErr\%, with the residual explained by known discretization effects
(Section~\ref{subsec:validation}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\sandtable{} draws on four bodies of work: mission engineering and the ProjectGL environment it
serves, human-machine teaming and supervisory control, agent-based combat simulation, and design of
experiments with optimization.

\emph{Mission engineering and ProjectGL.} Mission engineering analyzes how systems combine to
accomplish a mission and traces measures of effectiveness back to design choices
\citep{raz2024mission}. ProjectGL provides the Army's shared, modular basis for such analysis in the
ground domain \citep{stanko2025projectgl}. \sandtable{} does not replace it; it occupies the fast
end of a fidelity spectrum, producing the response surfaces that tell an engineer which designs are
worth running at high fidelity.

\emph{Human-machine teaming and supervisory control.} The distinction between direct and
supervisory control dates to early telerobotics \citep{sheridan1978supervisory}, and the limits of
a single operator's situation awareness \citep{endsley1995awareness} and attention set how many
assets that operator can effectively command. Operator-capacity models estimate this span from
task timing \citep{cummings2007capacity}, and reviews of human-agent teaming and multi-robot
supervision catalog the resulting interface and workload issues
\citep{chen2014teaming,chen2011supervisory}. \sandtable{} abstracts these findings into a
tractable queueing model of operator attention (Section~\ref{sec:model}) whose behavior we then
map across the span-by-communications design space. For uncrewed-aircraft swarms specifically, a
meta-analysis of swarm-management interfaces documents how operator performance degrades as swarm
size and tempo grow \citep{hocraffer2017meta}, which motivates our sensor-swarm study.

\emph{Agent-based combat simulation.} Low-fidelity, effect-based combat models have a long
lineage. Flocking rules generate formation movement from local interactions
\citep{reynolds1987flocks}; operation-time agent frameworks schedule swarm tasks against a mission
clock \citep{wei2013operation}; and general agent-based modeling frameworks such as Mesa
\citep{masad2015mesa} popularized the pattern we follow, though for a tight optimization loop we use
a bespoke vectorized state representation rather than per-agent Python objects. A recurring lesson
in this literature is to validate an emergent model against a closed-form law; we adopt Lanchester's
square law \citep{lanchester1916aircraft}, in the modern treatment of \citet{kress2020lanchester},
for that purpose.

\emph{Design of experiments and optimization.} The studies here are batch DOE sweeps; the same
plumbing supports Bayesian optimization via Monte-Carlo acquisition functions
\citep{balandat2020botorch} when a design space is too large to grid. The simulator core is built on
vectorized array programming \citep{harris2020numpy}.

\section{The \sandtable{} Model}
\label{sec:model}

\sandtable{} is a layered mission-level model: a pure-function evaluation contract wraps a
vectorized state and a fixed-step loop over movement, sensing, engagement, command, and
communications (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}). The subsections below describe each layer and the
two properties that make the model fit for a search loop.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{architecture.pdf}
\caption{\sandtable{} structure. A pure \texttt{run\_mission} builds the world (terrain rasters) and
the structure-of-arrays entity state from a scenario, seed, and design parameters, then advances a
fixed-step pipeline, command tasking, planning, motion, sensing, engagement, that reads and writes
that state each step, and returns scalar mission KPIs. \texttt{evaluate} averages the KPIs over
seeded replications, and a design-of-experiments and Bayesian-optimization orchestrator drives
batches on the HPC cluster.}
\label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Design contract}
The top-level interface is a pure function
\[
\texttt{run\_mission}(\text{scenario}, \text{seed}, \text{params}) \;\rightarrow\; \text{metrics},
\]
with no module-level state, no global random number generator, and no file or wall-clock access in
the hot path. A companion \texttt{evaluate} averages the metrics across a requested number of seeded
replications. This
boundary is what makes a run reproducible from its seed, cacheable, and safe to evaluate
concurrently, and it is what lets the optimizer treat the simulator as a black-box objective. All
randomness is drawn from a single generator seeded per replication, so identical seeds reproduce
identical metrics bit for bit, and matched seed blocks across paired studies implement common random
numbers for low-variance comparison.

\subsection{State and time}
Entities (ground vehicles, uncrewed aircraft, sensor and command nodes, and defenders) are stored
as a structure of arrays: position, heading, health, sensor and weapon parameters, and side live in
parallel NumPy arrays rather than in per-agent objects \citep{harris2020numpy}. The mission advances
on a fixed time step (1~s here). Each step updates, in order, command tasking, planning, motion,
sensing, and engagement (the pipeline of Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}). This vectorized design runs a full mission of up to 1800 steps in roughly
0.1~s on a single core, about 7 to 11 replications per second depending on scenario, so
the 44,100 replications behind this paper are one to two core-hours of compute, trivially parallel
across a cluster.

\subsection{Movement, sensing, and engagement}
\emph{Movement.} Ground platforms follow a unicycle kinematic model: each turns its heading toward
its current waypoint at a turn-rate limit and advances at
\[
v = v_{\max}\;\tau\;g(x,y)\;(0.35 + 0.65\,q),
\]
where $\tau$ is a scenario tempo factor, $g(x,y)\in[0,1]$ is the terrain trafficability sampled from
a precomputed raster (air platforms ignore it), and $q$ is the platform's control quality
(Section~\ref{subsec:command}), so a poorly controlled or stalled vehicle also loses tempo. Speed is
capped so a platform never oversteps its waypoint in one step. The waypoint comes from a
\texttt{route\_bias} parameter that sets a lateral lane between a fast exposed corridor and a slow
covered route, holds that lane through the threat, and converges onto the objective only over the
final stretch, so a covered route stays in defilade past the defenders.

\emph{Sensing and shared awareness.} Sensing is a cookie-cutter range gate feeding a per-side shared
picture. A target is placed on the opposing side's picture if any living observer holds it within
sensor range with clear line of sight, and then only with probability $1-c_{\text{conceal}}$ at the
target's location, so concealment lowers detectability. The picture is \emph{shared}: a contact any
friendly sensor holds is visible to every friendly shooter, which is what lets recon aircraft cue
ground vehicles that have not detected the threat themselves. A contact held \emph{only} by an air
sensor must be relayed, so under electronic warfare it is dropped from the shared picture with the
link's message-drop probability (Section~\ref{subsec:comms}); a contact also held organically by a
ground sensor is unaffected. Line of sight is a flat-terrain stub in the present model.

\emph{Engagement.} Engagement is aimed fire. Every living shooter engages its nearest detected enemy
within weapon range, and a kill is a Bernoulli draw with probability
\[
p_k = p_k^{\text{base}}\,(1 - c_{\text{cover}})\,q_{\text{shooter}}\,(2 - q_{\text{target}}),
\]
clipped to $[0,1]$, where $c_{\text{cover}}$ is terrain cover at the target: cover reduces incoming
lethality, and control quality couples both ways, since a well-controlled shooter fires more
effectively and a poorly controlled target exposes itself. Both sides fire against the same pre-step
state and damage is applied simultaneously, so there is no turn-order bias; recon aircraft carry no
weapon and are not targeted (no short-range air defense is modeled). This effect-based abstraction is
deliberately coarse; Section~\ref{subsec:validation} shows it still obeys the right aggregate law.

\subsection{Command model: direct versus supervisory}
\label{subsec:command}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{command_model.pdf}
\caption{The command model, the mechanism behind the moving optimum. Under \emph{direct} control an
asset requests a single shared operator over the comms/EW link and waits: the reply is high quality
when it arrives in time, low while the asset waits, and poor when the link drops it. Under
\emph{supervisory} control each asset decides locally at a steady autonomous quality, independent of
the link. More supervised assets both dilute the operator's per-decision quality and lengthen its
queue.}
\label{fig:command}
\end{figure}

The command layer is where the human-machine teaming questions live (Figure~\ref{fig:command}). In
\emph{supervisory} mode each asset runs on-platform autonomy at a steady control quality
$q_{\text{auto}}$, independent of communications. In \emph{direct} mode an asset requests the operator
over the modeled link, paying a round trip of $2\ell$ steps at one-way latency $\ell$ with per-message
drop probability $p$ (Table~\ref{tab:ladder}), and the single operator serves queued requests at a
fixed rate $\mu$. A request answered within the asset's patience returns the high operator quality
$q_{\text{operator}}$; while it waits the asset holds a low stall quality $q_{\text{stall}}$; and a
dropped or timed-out request drops the asset to a poor autonomous fallback $q_{\text{fallback}}$.
Crucially, the operator is a single server shared across all supervised assets. Attention
dilutes with span: the effective operator quality is
\[
q_{\text{operator}}^{\text{eff}} = q_{\text{auto}} + \big(q_{\text{operator}} - q_{\text{auto}}\big)\,
\min\!\Big(1, \tfrac{\kappa}{n}\Big),
\]
where $n$ is the number of supervised assets and $\kappa$ the operator's attention (span) capacity
(here $q_{\text{auto}}=0.70$, $q_{\text{operator}}=0.95$, $\kappa=4$). Below capacity the operator
adds full value; above it, requests queue and the human-in-the-loop advantage erodes. Control quality feeds engagement (a well-controlled shooter is more effective; a poorly
controlled or stalled asset exposes itself), so the abstract quality translates into mission
outcomes. This model, an operator as a capacity-limited server whose value is gated by both span
and link quality, is the mechanism behind the moving optimum of Section~\ref{subsec:centerpiece},
and it is grounded in the operator-capacity and supervisory-control literature
\citep{cummings2007capacity,chen2011supervisory}.

\subsection{Communications and EW ladder}
\label{subsec:comms}
A single \texttt{comms\_level} parameter indexes a C0--C5 degradation ladder, from clear (C0) to
severe (C5), that raises tasking latency and message-drop probability and thins the shared picture
by dropping relayed contacts (Table~\ref{tab:ladder}). The six rungs raise one-way tasking latency
from 0 to 16 time steps and message-drop probability from 0 to 0.55, spanning a benign link to a
heavily contested one; the rung values are set parameters, not measured link data. The same latency
$\ell$ and drop probability $p$ gate both the direct-control round trip (Section~\ref{subsec:command})
and the air-relay path in sensing, so a single dial degrades both tasking and the shared picture.
Because supervisory autonomy does not depend on the link while direct control does, the ladder is the
axis along which the two modalities separate.

\subsection{Engineered ground truth}
Every scenario carries a correct answer for the trial (which threats exist, which route survives,
what outcome a design should produce), and every agent exposes dial-able error and confidence. This
is what lets a study score a design against truth rather than against agreement with the model's own
agents, and it is a prerequisite for the pattern-detection objectives the broader program pursues.
Because each agent's error rate, confidence behavior, and version state are knobs rather than
emergent artifacts, a study can hold the ground truth fixed and vary only the teaming design under
test.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

We study three scenarios drawn from the program's use-case library (Figure~\ref{fig:scenarios};
platform parameters in Table~\ref{tab:platforms}). All three share a 6~km $\times$ 3~km area in which
a blue force spawns on the west edge and must move a surviving majority to an objective on the east
edge, held by static anti-tank (AT) teams. UC-3 (route choice) sweeps a ground formation's
\texttt{route\_bias} from a fast corridor to a covered defilade route. The centerpiece crosses
control modality (direct, supervisory) with span of control (one operator to $\{2,3,4,5,6,8\}$
assets) and the C0--C5 communications ladder, in a fair fight where red strength scales with blue.
UC-5 (sensor swarm) sweeps recon swarm size ($0$ to $\ucFiveSwarmMax{}$ aircraft) against the C0--C5
ladder, with the swarm cueing near-blind ground shooters against a defense in depth.

\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{scenarios.pdf}

The scenarios exercise different parts of the model. UC-3 turns on terrain and routing: the eight
ground vehicles have low lethality, so the decision is exposure against tempo, not firepower, and
\texttt{route\_bias} trades a short exposed path through the AT kill zone for a longer covered detour.
The centerpiece turns on the command layer: it fixes a moderately covered route and varies only the
teaming design (modality, span, communications), with the operator serving requests at rate
$\mu=0.15$ per step, span capacity $\kappa=4$, patience 20 steps, and a decision roughly every 22
steps. UC-5 turns on the air layer and the shared-picture relay: four ground vehicles whose sensor
range (350~m) is far shorter than their weapon range (1400~m) cannot see the threats they out-range,
so they depend on a UAS overwatch, laid out along the assault axis to blanket the depth of the
defense, and jamming degrades the relay that carries that picture. Table~\ref{tab:platforms} lists
the platform parameters for each scenario.

Each design point is averaged over \nRep{} Monte-Carlo replications. Seeds are derived so that
matching cells across the paired direct and supervisory studies share seed blocks, giving common
random numbers for the paired comparison. Studies are declared as YAML and executed by the DOE
orchestrator through its SLURM runner on the GMU Hopper cluster: one batch job per design point,
each running its \nRep{} replications. Reported outcomes are mission success rate (fraction of
replications meeting the objective), attrition (mean losses per side), time to objective, and, for
UC-5, detection coverage (the time-averaged fraction of the live threat force held on the shared
picture). Because a replication ends when the assault force reaches the objective or is eliminated,
coverage is averaged over an outcome-dependent window, so coverage and success are related, not
independent, measures. Success-rate confidence intervals are the normal approximation
$1.96\sqrt{p(1-p)/\nRep{}}$, about 6~percentage points at $p=0.5$.

All numbers and tables in this paper are generated from the study outputs by a single script and
inlined, so every value traces to code and data.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We first validate the attrition kernel against a closed-form law, then report the three studies:
route choice, the span-by-communications centerpiece, and the sensor swarm under EW.

\subsection{Validation against Lanchester's square law}
\label{subsec:validation}

Before drawing design conclusions we check that the attrition kernel obeys a recognized combat law.
\sandtable{} resolves fire as aimed fire, so when fire is distributed the kill rate on a side is
proportional to the number of opposing shooters, which is the hypothesis of Lanchester's square law
\citep{lanchester1916aircraft}. For initial blue and red strengths $B_0$ and $R_0$ of equal
per-unit effectiveness, the stronger side annihilates the weaker and its survivors are
$\sqrt{B_0^2 - R_0^2}$. We isolate the kernel (no movement, no cover, mutual
detection, forces intermixed so fire distributes) and compare the Monte-Carlo mean survivors to that
closed form (Table~\ref{tab:lanchester}). Agreement is within \lanchMaxErr\%, biased slightly low.
The bias is expected and conservative: simultaneous discrete-time resolution lets a unit about to
die still return fire (an effect that vanishes as the per-step kill probability shrinks), and
nearest-target assignment produces occasional overkill. A low-fidelity model reproducing the square
law to within a few percent is the defensibility check this class of model calls for.

\label{subsec:uc3}

\subsection{The moving optimum: span of control under EW}
\label{subsec:centerpiece}

The centerpiece study crosses control modality with span of control and the communications ladder.
Figure~\ref{fig:cp_curves} shows the mechanism directly. Supervisory autonomy is nearly
communications-invariant: at a span of one operator to two assets its success stays between
\cpSupLoSpanMin\% and \cpSupLoSpanMax\% across the entire ladder (a spread of \cpSupLoSpanSpread{}
percentage points). Direct control degrades along two axes at once. It falls with communications,
from \cpDirClearLoSpan\% at C0 to \cpDirSevereLoSpan\% at C5 for the one-to-two span, and it falls
with span, because the single operator saturates: at one-to-eight, direct control is already floored
at \cpDirClearHiSpan\% even in clear communications, against \cpSupClearHiSpan\% for supervisory.

Figure~\ref{fig:cp_phase} collapses the full design space into a single decision aid. Each cell is
the supervisory-minus-direct success difference; blue cells (bottom-left, low span and clear
communications) are where a human in the loop wins, red cells everywhere else are where on-platform
autonomy wins, and the black contour is the crossover. The optimum \emph{moves}. At the smallest
span the crossover sits at C$\cpStarLoSpan{}$: direct control is competitive only while
communications are near-clear. As span grows the crossover slides left until, at one-to-eight,
supervisory autonomy dominates at every communications level (crossover C$\cpStarHiSpan{}$), by
\cpDeltaHiSpanClear{}~percentage points even in the clear. Across the whole grid the supervisory
advantage reaches \cpDeltaMax{}~percentage points, at span one-to-six under the heaviest
degradation. The single most decision-relevant reading is the boundary
itself: the more assets one operator must supervise, the less communications degradation it takes
before control should be handed to the machines. Table~\ref{tab:centerpiece} gives the full grid.

\subsection{Sensor swarm under electronic warfare}
\label{subsec:uc5}

UC-5 pairs a recon swarm with near-blind ground shooters against a defense in depth, then jams the
link. Figure~\ref{fig:uc5} and Table~\ref{tab:uc5} give the result. With no recon the shooters
cannot see the threats that out-range them and the assault fails, succeeding only
\ucFiveSuccBlind\% of the time. Adding aircraft builds detection coverage with a clear threshold:
coverage climbs steeply until the swarm is large enough to blanket the depth of the defense (near
\ucFiveCovBestClear{} at the largest swarm in clear conditions) and mission success rises with it,
to \ucFiveSuccBestClear\%. The sensor-to-shooter chain is the whole story: recon is what lets blind
shooters engage, and swarm size buys the coverage that cues them.

Jamming erodes that chain. As jamming intensifies it thins the shared picture, and both coverage and
success fall sharply at every swarm size, though they stay well above the no-recon baseline. At the
largest swarm, success falls from \ucFiveSuccBestClear\% in clear conditions to
\ucFiveSuccBestSevere\% under severe EW, and coverage from \ucFiveCovBestClear{} to
\ucFiveCovBestSevere{}; a mid-sized swarm falls from \ucFiveSuccMidClear\% to \ucFiveSuccMidSevere\%,
roughly halving. The design implication is that swarm size and
communications resilience are complements, not substitutes: buying more aircraft raises the ceiling
in clear conditions but does not protect the picture once the link is contested.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

All three studies make the same point in different settings: the best design is not fixed but
\emph{contingent} on the contested-conditions axis, and \sandtable{} locates where it flips. Route
choice prices tempo against survivability so a planner can pick a point on the frontier to match the
mission. The centerpiece turns span of control and communications state into a single rule: hand
control to autonomy sooner as either span or degradation grows, and the phase diagram says exactly
when. The swarm study shows that sensing capacity and link resilience must be bought together,
because EW attacks the chain that connects them. None of these is a claim about a single design; each
shows how the right choice shifts as conditions change, which is the question a mission engineer
actually has to answer.

Methodologically, the results argue for the two-tier workflow. \sandtable{} ran 44,100
replications in one to two core-hours and returned smooth, well-ordered response surfaces with tight
confidence intervals, precisely the regions and boundaries a high-fidelity ProjectGL study should
then confirm. The fast tier is not a replacement for fidelity; it is what makes fidelity affordable,
by spending the expensive engine only on the designs the cheap one has flagged.


\sandtable{} is deliberately low-fidelity, and its conclusions inherit that. Movement is 2.5D
kinematic, not physics-based; sensing and engagement are effect-based draws, not signal or
signature models; terrain is a scalar trafficability-and-cover field, not a rendered environment;
and line of sight is a flat-terrain stub. Tactical behavior is simplified too: red forces are static
defenders that fire but neither maneuver nor react, so every finding is conditioned on a
non-adaptive threat, and recon aircraft cannot be engaged (no air defense is modeled), so swarm size
carries no survivability cost and the UC-5 upside is an upper bound.

The model's parameters are set, not measured. The command model is a capacity-limited-server
abstraction of operator attention, calibrated to the qualitative findings of the supervisory-control
literature rather than to human-subject data; and the lethality probabilities and the C0--C5 latency
and drop values are hand-set, so they, not measured link or weapon data, fix where the crossover
falls and how fast the sensor-to-shooter chain degrades. The studies also use fixed heuristic
behaviors rather than learned or reinforcement-learning policies, a single operator with no handoff
or dynamic span reallocation, and three scenarios with small teams; extrapolation to larger forces,
multi-operator command, or adaptive policies is untested.

The absolute success rates should therefore be read as comparative, not predictive: the value is in
the \emph{ordering} of designs and the \emph{location} of crossovers, which is what a search loop
consumes, not in any single percentage. The moving optimum's exact crossover cells near the contour
are within Monte-Carlo noise, so what the study establishes is the direction of the boundary, not
the precise cell. The Lanchester check validates the aggregate attrition law but not the spatial or command
dynamics, which is why the intended role of the model is to feed, not replace, high-fidelity
validation.

We built \sandtable{}, a fast, reproducible, pure-Python mission-level testbed, as the inner-loop
companion to the Army's high-fidelity ProjectGL, and used it to produce three decision-relevant
maps for human-machine teaming under contested communications: a moving direct-to-supervisory
control optimum, a speed-survivability frontier for route choice, and the EW-induced collapse of a
sensor-to-shooter chain. The tool obeys a recognized attrition law, runs a full mission in about a
tenth of a second, and plugs directly into a Bayesian-optimization and DOE orchestrator, so the
next step is to search these design spaces with optimization rather than grids, and to confirm the
flagged designs in ProjectGL.

\subsection{A prototype belief layer}
\label{subsec:belief}

Beyond the three studies, we prototyped the single extension that most changes what the testbed can
study: a first-class belief layer. The baseline carries perception as one bit, a target is on the
shared picture or it is not, and a shooter fires against its true position, so electronic warfare can
only thin the picture and deception has nowhere to act. The prototype instead gives each side a
persistent \emph{track} per opposing entity: a believed position that is refreshed on detection and
goes stale, frozen with decaying confidence, when detection lapses, plus optional \emph{false tracks}
that model decoys and spoofing. Fires resolve against the believed position and its confidence rather
than truth, so a jammed link lets a track drift and miss, and a decoy draws a wasted shot. The layer
is opt-in and off by default, so every number reported above is unchanged. Figure~\ref{fig:tick}
places this state beside the baseline and shows the fixed-order per-step pipeline it plugs into.

The effect is first-order. On UC-3, mean blue losses over \beliefNRep{} replications are
\beliefBaseLoss{} for the baseline seen-bit model and \beliefCleanLoss{} for the belief model in
clear communications, so with fresh tracks the two nearly coincide. Adding decoys drops blue losses
to \beliefDecoyLoss{}, because spoofed tracks pull fire off the real force, an effect a
probability-of-kill modifier cannot produce because it needs a belief that deception can corrupt.
Severe jamming drops them to \beliefJamLoss{} by a different route, stale tracks whose believed
positions drift from the moving truth, so the jammed side misses more often. Electronic warfare and
deception therefore act on outcomes through the belief state, first-order, rather than as modifier
tables, which is how engagement-level frameworks such as AFSIM allocate fires against tracks rather
than truth. This is the most decision-relevant step toward the program's deception and
trust-calibration objectives.

\subsection{Extensions}
\label{subsec:extensions}

The cheapest gains come from hardening the findings the model already produces. A global sensitivity
analysis over the operator parameters (service rate, attention capacity, and patience) and the
C0--C5 latency and drop values, using Morris screening or Sobol indices, would report each crossover
as a band with a variance decomposition that says which inputs actually move it. That converts the
moving optimum from a point estimate, whose exact cells sit within Monte-Carlo noise, into a boundary
with stated confidence, and it names the few parameters worth measuring. Two calibration steps would
supply those measurements: fitting the command model to human-subject supervisory-control data rather
than the qualitative anchor used here \citep{cummings2007capacity,chen2011supervisory,hocraffer2017meta},
and Bayesian calibration of the lethality and link parameters against high-fidelity ProjectGL runs
\citep{stanko2025projectgl}, so the fast tier inherits the engine's numbers where they set the
crossover.

Richer mechanisms would close the fidelity gaps that bound the present conclusions. Replacing the
binary range gate and the flat line-of-sight stub with a range-dependent detection probability, true
line-of-sight on the trafficability grid, and a false-alarm rate would sharpen the recon-cueing chain
that drives the swarm study and the shared picture in the centerpiece. A reactive red force that
maneuvers, seeks cover, and counters reconnaissance would lift every finding off the assumption of a
static defender, and adding short-range air defense would give swarm size a survivability cost and
remove the upper bound on the UC-5 result. Extending the single operator to a network of servers with
dynamic span reallocation and handoff would turn the span-of-control finding from a fixed ratio into a
staffing and handoff policy, which is closer to how a command post is actually manned.

The largest extensions point the testbed at the program's learning and analytics objectives. Swapping
the scripted heuristics for reinforcement-learning and imitation policies would test the
direct-versus-supervisory tradeoff on adaptive teaming behavior rather than fixed rules, and would let
the same policies transfer to the Learning Agents runtime inside ProjectGL. A deep-learning surrogate
with uncertainty quantification, fitted to the response surfaces and queried by the
Bayesian-optimization loop \citep{balandat2020botorch} with an acquisition rule that targets
high-variance regions, would cut the number of simulator calls each search needs. Building on the
belief-layer prototype (Section~\ref{subsec:belief}), broadening the
use-case library toward the deception and trust cases in the design, spoofed contacts, thermal decoys,
and confident-but-wrong recommendations, would exercise the engineered-ground-truth machinery for the
trust-calibration metric and let pattern-mining over the run logs surface the success and failure
signatures the program set out to detect.
